DIRECTOR ORSZAG:\xc2\xa0 Good morning.\xc2\xa0 Thank you for joining us.\xc2\xa0 I\'m Peter Orszag, Director of the Office of Management and Budget.\xc2\xa0 I\'ll give a brief overview and then I will turn it over to my colleague, Christy Romer, who is the Chair of the Council of Economic Advisers, to describe in a bit more detail the economics behind the fiscal year 2011 budget, which we are releasing this morning.

\n

That fiscal year 2011 budget focuses on three things:\xc2\xa0 job creation, middle class security, and putting the nation back on a path to fiscal sustainability.\xc2\xa0 Before turning to the details of the budget let me just give a little bit of context and background.

\n

We just came through a year in which a second Great Depression was averted.\xc2\xa0 At the end of 2008, real GDP was declining by more than 5 percent on an annualized basis.\xc2\xa0 At the end of 2009, it was increasing by more than 5 percent on an annualized basis.\xc2\xa0 Although real GDP in the economy is now expanding, the employment market remains too weak.\xc2\xa0 The unemployment rate is 10 percent and there are now 7 million fewer jobs than in December 2007.

\n

That\'s why this budget includes important investments to spur job creation now, including the jobs and wages tax credit that the President spoke about last week, and including key investments in the drivers of longer-term economic growth -- education, innovation, and moving toward a clean energy future.

\n

Now, let\'s also do a little bit of background and context on the budget.\xc2\xa0 In January 2009, the situation that was apparent at that point involved two key features that are still the case.\xc2\xa0 One was a significant increase in spending from 2008 to 2009, and the second was significant out-year deficits.\xc2\xa0 Both of those were already apparent before the administration took office.

\n

In particular, on the first case, in January 2009, several weeks before the administration took office, the Congressional Budget Office issued an economic and budget outlook.\xc2\xa0 That economic and budget outlook showed an increase in spending as a share of the economy from 20.9 percent in fiscal year 2008 to 24.9 percent in 2009.\xc2\xa0 The reality actually came in slightly lower than that, 24.7 [percent].\xc2\xa0 But the point was in a slightly different mix -- less mandatory spending, more discretionary spending, the point was that significant increase in spending as a share of the economy was already apparent at the end of 2008 and in early 2009.

\n

Second, we also faced not only a 2009 deficit that was in excess of $1.3 trillion, but a medium-term deficit in excess of $8 trillion.\xc2\xa0 And that came from two primary sources.\xc2\xa0 The first was a set of policies -- the 2001 and 2003 tax cuts and the Medicare prescription drug benefit -- that had not been paid for -- that amounts to almost $6 trillion with added interest costs over the next decade because those policies were not offset.

\n

And second, the effects of the economic downturn amounted to about $2.5 trillion because when the economy weakens, tax revenue declines and certain types of spending automatically increase.\xc2\xa0 Those automatic stabilizers again added about $2.5 trillion to the projected deficit over the decade.

\n

Now, that is an explanation of the situation in which we found ourselves and in which we continue to find ourselves, but it does not explain the path forward.\xc2\xa0 So what in particular are we doing to bring the deficit down from roughly 10 percent of the economy in 2009?\xc2\xa0 Several things.

\n

The first step is to make sure you don\'t dig the hole deeper.\xc2\xa0 We are particularly pleased that the Senate has now joined the House in embracing a proposal that the administration embraces, so-called statutory pay-as-you-go legislation.\xc2\xa0 That is to embed in law the common sense principle that any new proposals or new tax cuts need to be offset so that they don\'t cause a deterioration in the budget deficit.\xc2\xa0 If we had lived by this simple principle over the past several years, we would be facing out-year deficits that would be roughly 2 percent of the economy, and debt as a share of the economy would be declining rather than rising.

\n

Second, the economic recovery, which will ultimately take hold, will help to reduce the deficit from about 10 percent of the economy down to about 5 percent of the economy by 2015, because tax revenue will recover and those automatic stabilizers on the spending side will also decline.

\n

Our goal is roughly 3 percent of the economy, which would balance the budget, excluding interest payments on the debt.\xc2\xa0 To get from 5 [percent] to 3 [percent], we have a series of proposals.\xc2\xa0 The first is that we put forward specific steps to reduce the deficit over the next decade by $1.2 trillion, which would move us from 5 percent of the economy in 2015 to 4 percent.\xc2\xa0 These steps include measures such as the financial services fee, which raises $90 billion over a decade, helps to repay taxpayers for the full cost of TARP, and also by imposing the tax on our largest financial institutions, helps to discourage the leverage that was partly the cause of the financial meltdown that we have experienced.

\n

Second, we allow the 2001 and 2003 tax cuts to expire for those with incomes above $250,000 in 2011 and thereafter, as scheduled under the legislation.\xc2\xa0 That reduces the deficit by almost $700 billion over the next decade.

\n

Third, as part of our effort to move towards a clean energy future, we eliminate fossil fuel subsidies, which reduces the deficit by about $40 billion over the next decade.

\n

And then in addition to all of that, we have a three-year freeze on non-security discretionary spending, which will reduce the deficit by $250 billion.\xc2\xa0 Now, let me be very clear about this freeze.\xc2\xa0 First, it is not across the board.\xc2\xa0 Some agencies and programs are going up, and other agencies and programs are going down.\xc2\xa0 In particular, we\'re investing an additional $3 billion in education, because that\'s one of the keys to future economic prosperity, even while we\'re reforming those programs -- for example, taking 38 existing, duplicative programs, consolidating them into 11, and putting more emphasis on results rather than just funding.

\n

Second, we are investing significantly in research and development, including more than $61 billion in fiscal year 2011, which is up 6 percent, because research and development and innovation are also key drivers of economic activity.

\n

And third, we are investing in clean energy -- more than $6 billion to move towards the energy sources of the future, working in concert with elimination of fossil fuel subsidies so that we can become the world\'s leader in green energy.

\n

All of these together move the deficit in 2015 to roughly 4 percent of the economy, which as I\'ve said is above where we\'d like to be.\xc2\xa0 To get the rest of the way there will require a bipartisan process, which is why we are calling for a bipartisan fiscal commission tasked not only with addressing our long-term fiscal imbalance, but also producing plans that will balance the budget, excluding interest payments on the debt, by 2015.

\n

Finally, let me note that that all has to do with deficit over the next decade, making key investments in job creation today, reducing the deficit by $1.2 trillion over the next decade, while investing in education, innovation, and clean energy.

\n

In addition to that set of challenges, we also face a long-term fiscal gap, which is driven primarily by health care costs.\xc2\xa0 And one of the reasons why the administration has been so focused on comprehensive health reform legislation is that it would not only reduce the deficit over the next decade, but perhaps as importantly or even more importantly put in place the infrastructure and the policies that will help to constrain costs and improve quality in the decades thereafter.\xc2\xa0 And unless we do that, we will continue to face a very substantial long-term fiscal gap.

\n

Well, there\'s certainly no question that the past year has been an incredibly difficult one for the American economy and for the American people.\xc2\xa0 Because of the actions taken over the past year, the trajectory of the economy is greatly improved.\xc2\xa0 As Peter mentioned, real GDP expanded strongly in the fourth quarter of 2009, and shows every sign of continuing to grow steadily.

\n

However, as our forecast makes clear, the path back to full employment will take time and continued vigilance.\xc2\xa0 The President is committed to taking every responsible action to accelerate job creation and speed recovery.
