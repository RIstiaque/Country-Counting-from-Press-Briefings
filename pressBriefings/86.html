\n\tQ\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 Okay, as we\'re talking about trying to secure nuclear and radiological material, can you just give us a sense of what you have as the biggest concern?\xc2\xa0 How much of it is there?\xc2\xa0 Where is it?\xc2\xa0 Is industry doing enough to secure radiological material? \n\tMR. RHODES:\xc2\xa0 The summit process that we\'ve initiated since 2009 aims to get at the challenge of securing nuclear materials in a number of different ways.\xc2\xa0 Number one, we\'ve aimed to reduce significantly the amount of dangerous nuclear materials.\xc2\xa0 And so you\xe2\x80\x99ve had a significant reduction in the amount of highly enriched uranium and plutonium around the world through this summit process.\xc2\xa0 You\xe2\x80\x99ve had a significant number of countries who\xe2\x80\x99ve eliminated HEU from their shores and the overall amount of HEU come down by a substantial percentage around the world.

\n

\n\tAt the same time, we want to have better security measures in place at nuclear facilities, so that essentially nations can share information about how they can best protect this material. And you want to have better international cooperation among nations and multilateral organizations that are charged with the responsibility of securing these materials.\xc2\xa0 We also want to have enhanced detection for nuclear materials at ports of entry and other places of transit so that we can monitor whether or not there\'s an attempt to smuggle this material.

\n

\n\tSo there\'s been a multi-faceted effort to reduce the risk in terms of the amount of this material, to better secure it, and to guard against it being able to be transferred.\xc2\xa0

\n

\n\tIn terms of the scenarios, first of all, there are two different I think challenges that often get talked about when it comes to terrorists.\xc2\xa0 One is the most dangerous and highest-consequence threat, which would be terrorists actually obtaining either a device or material that could be used for a nuclear-yield explosion.\xc2\xa0 And that would get at, again, being able to have terrorists get their hands on very dangerous nuclear material and determine a way to weaponized that.\xc2\xa0 That is obviously an extraordinarily difficult thing to do, and we have made it much more difficult over the summit process.

\n

\n\tAnd then there\'s the more rudimentary dirty bomb, where you would seek to release radioactive materials not through a nuclear yield but still, of course, having great public health effects if done so in a way that caused a significant radiological explosion.\xc2\xa0 And there, too, we\'ve aimed to increase the security practices around how those materials are protected.

\n

\n\tI think the Belgium example -- I think it reinforces what we\'ve seen for many years, which is that we have seen indications, both through their public statements and through their actions, that terrorist organizations like al Qaeda and ISIL have an interest in getting their hands on these types of materials.\xc2\xa0 They want to do as much damage as possible.\xc2\xa0 That was al Qaeda\'s position for many years; we have no reason to doubt that that is ISIL\'s position as well.

\n

\n\tAt the same time, a country like Belgium does have very substantial nuclear security.\xc2\xa0 So this is a highly advanced democracy with longstanding experience in this field, so they have very high standards of nuclear security.\xc2\xa0

\n

\n\tFrankly, what we are aiming to do through the summit process is bring the standard up around the world so that it is at the level that we see certainly here in the United States and among some of our close allies.\xc2\xa0 So I don\'t think we believed that there was a significant risk in that scenario, however, it could indicate that this is an area that ISIL is looking into, just as we\'ve seen al Qaeda do that in the past.
