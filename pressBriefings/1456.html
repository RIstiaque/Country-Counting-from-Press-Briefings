PRESS BRIEFING BY WHITE HOUSE PRESS SECRETARY ROBERT GIBBS, DEPUTY NATIONAL SECURITY ADVISOR FOR INTERNATIONAL ECONOMIC AFFAIRS MICHAEL FROMAN, AND DEPUTY NATIONAL SECURITY ADVISOR FOR STRATEGIC COMMUNICATIONS BEN RHODES ON THE PRESIDENT\'S TRIP TO SINGAPORE AND THE APEC SUMMIT

\n

\xc2\xa012:57 P.M. JST

\n

MR. GIBBS:\xc2\xa0 Hello, everyone.\xc2\xa0 I\'ll take a few questions after this.\xc2\xa0 We\'ve got joining us today Michael Froman, Deputy National Security Advisor for International Economic Affairs, to walk you through a little preview of APEC; and Ben Rhodes, if there are any lingering questions from today\'s speech.\xc2\xa0 And I\'ll give it to them.

\n

MR. FROMAN:\xc2\xa0 Thank you.\xc2\xa0 Good morning -- good afternoon.\xc2\xa0 We leave this afternoon for Singapore, as you know.\xc2\xa0 The President will get there, we expect, in time to join the tail end of the dinner that the leaders are having.\xc2\xa0 Tomorrow morning he will have meetings with the Prime Minister of Singapore, Mr. Lee.\xc2\xa0 He\'ll join the leaders\' retreat, which is a two-hour discussion largely around -- the agenda intends to be around balanced and sustainable growth.\xc2\xa0 Then in the afternoon, I think as you know, he\'ll see the Indonesian President, the Russian President, and the ASEAN 10 -- the first time that a U.S. President has met with the ASEAN 10.

\n

The purpose of the trip to APEC, as the President said in his speech today, is to engage with what is an important region to the U.S. economy; about 60 percent of U.S. exports currently go to APEC countries, 26 percent of which go to the Asian part of APEC.\xc2\xa0 That accounts for 3.7 million jobs in the United States.\xc2\xa0 And for every increase -- every percentage increase in the share of exports to this region going forward it could create an estimated quarter of a million jobs back in the United States.

\n

So engagement with the APEC countries around regional economic creation and trade liberalization is a key part of our economic strategy back home to strengthen exports and build export-related jobs in the United States.

\n

The APEC agenda is one that includes both a discussion of balanced and sustainable and inclusive growth, as well as trade liberalization.\xc2\xa0 We expect that the APEC countries will embrace some of the outcomes of the G20 summit in Pittsburgh and talk about how they might apply them in this region.\xc2\xa0 And we expect that there will be a discussion of trade liberalization including the Doha Round and the President\'s efforts, as he said in his speech, to see whether we can achieve a balanced and ambitious conclusion to those negotiations.

\n

With that, why don\'t I stop and --

Q\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 Mike, can you tell us what the President meant by he "wants to engage the Trans-Pacific Partnership"?\xc2\xa0 Does he mean join, negotiate?

\n

MR. FROMAN:\xc2\xa0 I think what he meant is that there is an ongoing initiative called the Trans-Pacific Partnership and that our intent is to engage with them to see whether we can shape that initiative into one that is comprehensive and a very high standard and could serve as a platform for further trade liberalization and regional integration in the region.\xc2\xa0 We\'ll begin those discussions with the current and potential future members of the Trans-Pacific Partnership, and see whether this is something that could prove to be an important platform going forward.

\n

Q\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 Could one of you please just elaborate on the change in the schedule?

\n

MR. GIBBS:\xc2\xa0 Just quickly, we had built into the schedule a bit of extra time and some downtime, and the President decided very early this morning that he thought it was more important to get to APEC earlier and be part of the discussions tonight.\xc2\xa0 And so we made that change.

\n

Yes, sir.

Q\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 Ben, could you explain what the change is in Burma?

\n

MR. RHODES:\xc2\xa0 Well, I think that, as the President outlined it today, the change is essentially that you\'ve had a period of time where we had sanctions applied and no engagement with the government, and engagement kind of came through other channels.\xc2\xa0 So the change is really combining direct engagement with the Burmese government, with the ongoing sanctions regime, and laying out for them in very clear terms, as the President did today, what they need to do to have a better relationship with the United States.\xc2\xa0

\n

And Assistant Secretary Campbell was there recently, met with Aung San Suu Kyi, and the government as well, as a part of this attempt to press them on these issues through engagement while maintaining a robust sanctions regime.

\n

So the change is just combining engagement with sanctions and putting forward in very clear terms what the steps are that they would have to take in order to have a better relationship with the United States.

This meeting is of course taking place in that context, so it\'s\xc2\xa0 -- it was an opportunity since Russia will be at APEC to -- for them to get together.\xc2\xa0 So I think they\'ll be looking to discuss how to move that forward, yes.

Q\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 Ben, can I follow up on that?\xc2\xa0 It\'s pretty clear that they\'re not meeting to finalize any last-minute agreements or understandings to put the new START together.\xc2\xa0 So are they trying to talk about ways to have an umbrella agreement to get you past December 5th in a way that allows the negotiations to continue?

Q\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 Michael, the President talked in his speech about free trade, economic engagement with the region.\xc2\xa0 How does the South Korean free trade agreement fit into that?\xc2\xa0 And does the administration have any even rough timeline when it would like to see that resolved and put forward and submitted to Congress and moved through that in a way that makes it a real deal?

MR. FROMAN:\xc2\xa0 I think the President mentioned in his speech that he looked forward to working with our South Korean friends to address the outstanding issues so that we could move forward a trade agreement with them.\xc2\xa0 That will be, I\'m sure, part of the discussion with the South Koreans both in APEC and when we\'re in Seoul early next week, including with regard to the timetable.

Q\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0\xc2\xa0 The accent the President put on being the first Pacific President and the fact that America is a Pacific nation -- doesn\'t this send a powerful message that Europe is not really high on his priorities?

MR. GIBBS:\xc2\xa0 From the Italian press.

\n

MR. RHODES:\xc2\xa0 No, absolutely not.\xc2\xa0 The President is very, very deeply committed to the transatlantic alliance and our European allies.\xc2\xa0 He\'s obviously traveled extensively in Europe already this year.\xc2\xa0 As the President often likes to say, the world is not made up of zero-sum relationships.\xc2\xa0 And one of the central tenets of his foreign policy is that we need not ignore one part of the world to pay attention to another.\xc2\xa0

\n

So certainly not.\xc2\xa0 On the Pacific point, though, I think there are a number of points I\'d underscore that he included in the speech.\xc2\xa0 One, he was born in the Pacific, in Hawaii.\xc2\xa0 He grew up in Indonesia.\xc2\xa0 His mother worked extensively in the region.\xc2\xa0 He\'s traveled extensively in the region as a young man.\xc2\xa0 So this is a part of the world that helped shape his worldview.

\n

And then in terms of the United States, as Mike pointed out, we have a very strong series of relationships in both the economic side and the security side in this part of the world.\xc2\xa0 I think the President is sending both today in his speech and through this trip a very strong signal that we intend to be deeply engaged in this part of the world, both through our bilateral relationships, through some of these multilateral organizations that, frankly, the United States had not been as engaged in in recent years; and that that is based upon the deep interconnection that we have with the Asia Pacific region and the very extensive common interests that we hold.

\n

But I would -- in no way would that be at the expense of any other part of the world.\xc2\xa0 Again, the President rejects the notion that there is a zero-sum equation that if you are focused on one thing you can\'t also be deeply engaged in another.
